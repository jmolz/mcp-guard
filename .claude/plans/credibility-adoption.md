```markdown
## Cross-Validation

We investigated cross-validating MCP-Guard against GenTelLab's MCP-AttackBench
(the largest published MCP security dataset at 70K+ samples). The dataset is
incompatible with protocol-layer evaluation:

- **96.77% of samples are jailbreak/prompt injection strings** — flat natural
  language text designed for agent-layer text classification, not structured
  JSON-RPC messages
- **Threat model mismatch** — MCP-AttackBench evaluates whether a model can be
  tricked into making malicious calls. MCP-Guard evaluates whether a malicious
  call (regardless of origin) gets blocked at the protocol layer. These are
  complementary defenses at different layers, not competing approaches to the
  same problem.

  MCP tool_call payloads, permission probes, or PII exfiltration attempts in
  protocol message format

MCP-Guard's benchmark suite remains self-generated. We mitigate this with:
open-source reproducibility (`pnpm benchmark`), programmatic generation with
documented methodology, per-category transparency (showing 92.4%–100% range
rather than a single aggregate), and explicit limitations disclosure.

We welcome external benchmark suites targeting protocol-layer MCP security.
If one emerges, we will cross-validate and publish results.
```

## Notes

### Design Decisions

- **MCP-AttackBench cross-validation → incompatibility documentation**: Research showed 96.77% of samples are jailbreak prompts (agent-layer text classification), full dataset unreleased, and samples are flat strings not JSON-RPC messages. Documenting the investigation and explaining *why* it doesn't apply is a stronger credibility signal than a forced integration — it demonstrates we understand the architectural layers and are transparent about self-benchmarking limits.

- **Single entry for awesome-mcp-security**: The original plan proposed two entries (tool + benchmark suite). Research showed the maintainer merges PRs in batches and simpler submissions have higher merge rates. The benchmark is discoverable from the repo README.

- **Scope section separate from Limitations**: The benchmark "Limitations" subsection is about test methodology gaps. The new "Scope" section is about what the tool architecturally does and doesn't cover. These are distinct concepts and readers have different questions when they encounter each.

- **VHS for demo recording**: Charm's VHS is declarative (tape files), reproducible, and produces clean GIFs without manual recording. Fallback to SVG diagram if VHS isn't available — lower impact but zero risk of broken tooling.

- **No specific competitor mentions in Scope section**: Referencing "agent-layer defenses" generically rather than naming Invariant Guardrails or GenTelLab's tool. Avoids endorsing competitors and future-proofs against tool renames/deprecations.

### Risks

- **awesome-mcp-security PR may not be merged**: The maintainer has 54 open issues and merges in batches. The PR may sit for weeks. This is fine — the submission signals community engagement regardless.
- **Demo GIF quality**: Terminal recordings can look janky if timing is off. VHS mitigates this with scripted timing, but the fallback SVG is safer.
- **Scope section tone**: Easy to slip into apologetic language. The contract includes a negative criterion checking for hedging words.
